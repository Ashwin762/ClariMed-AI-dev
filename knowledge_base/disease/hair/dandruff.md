# Dandruff

## Overview

Dandruff is a common scalp condition causing white or greyish flakes of dead skin, often with itching. It's not contagious and rarely serious, but it can be persistent and cosmetically bothersome.

---

# Common Symptoms

- White or greyish flakes on the scalp, hair, and shoulders
- Itchy scalp
- Mild scalp redness in some cases
- Flaking that worsens in dry or cold weather

---

# Causes

### Malassezia yeast overgrowth

A naturally occurring scalp yeast that, in some people, triggers irritation and increased skin cell turnover.

### Other contributors

Oily scalp, dry skin, sensitivity to hair products, and infrequent shampooing.

---

# Risk Factors

- Oily scalp and hair
- Stress
- Cold, dry weather
- Certain neurological conditions (Parkinson's)
- Weakened immune system

# Severity Classification

## 🟢 Low

- Occasional light flaking
- Minimal itching

Recommendation: Regular shampooing with an anti-dandruff shampoo.

---

## 🟡 Moderate

- Persistent heavy flaking
- Noticeable itching or scalp redness
- Not improving with over-the-counter shampoos

Recommendation: Consult a dermatologist — a medicated treatment may be needed.

---

# Home Care

- Shampoo regularly with an anti-dandruff shampoo (containing zinc pyrithione, ketoconazole, or selenium sulfide).
- Massage shampoo gently into the scalp and leave for a few minutes before rinsing.
- Avoid harsh styling products.
- Manage stress levels.

---

# Prevention

- Maintain a consistent hair-washing routine.
- Rotate between different anti-dandruff shampoo types if one loses effectiveness.
- Avoid excessive heat styling.
- Eat a balanced diet.

---

# When to Consult a Doctor

Consult a dermatologist if:

- Flaking persists despite several weeks of anti-dandruff shampoo
- The scalp becomes very red, sore, or swollen
- Flaking spreads to the face, eyebrows, or chest

---

# Emergency Warning Signs

Dandruff is not an emergency. However, seek care if signs of scalp infection develop (pus, spreading redness, significant pain, or fever).
