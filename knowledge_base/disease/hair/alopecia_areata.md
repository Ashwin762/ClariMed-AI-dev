# Alopecia Areata

## Overview

Alopecia areata is an autoimmune condition causing sudden, patchy hair loss — typically smooth, round bald patches on the scalp. It's not contagious, and hair often regrows on its own, though the course is unpredictable.

---

# Common Symptoms

- Sudden appearance of smooth, round bald patches
- Patches with no scaling, redness, or scarring
- Occasional tingling or mild discomfort before hair falls out
- Nail pitting (in some cases)
- Hair loss may also affect eyebrows or beard

---

# Causes

### Autoimmune process

The immune system mistakenly attacks hair follicles, halting hair growth without permanently destroying the follicle.

---

# Risk Factors

- Family history of alopecia areata
- Personal or family history of other autoimmune conditions (thyroid disease, vitiligo)
- Significant stress (may act as a trigger)

# Severity Classification

## 🟢 Low

- One or two small patches
- No spreading

Recommendation: Consult a dermatologist for confirmation; many cases regrow on their own.

---

## 🟡 Moderate

- Multiple or spreading patches
- Loss affecting eyebrows, beard, or large scalp areas

Recommendation: Consult a dermatologist to discuss treatment options.

---

# Home Care

- No home treatment reverses alopecia areata — dermatology guidance is needed for treatment options.
- Gentle hair care; avoid harsh chemical treatments on affected areas.
- Protect exposed scalp from sun.
- Stress management may help, though it doesn't cure the condition.

---

# Prevention

There is no known way to prevent alopecia areata, as it's an autoimmune condition. Early dermatology consultation helps identify treatment options and rule out other causes of hair loss.

---

# When to Consult a Doctor

Consult a dermatologist if:

- You notice sudden patchy hair loss
- Patches are spreading or increasing in number
- Hair loss affects eyebrows, eyelashes, or beard
- You have accompanying nail changes

---

# Emergency Warning Signs

Alopecia areata is not an emergency. However, since it's linked to other autoimmune conditions, significant fatigue, weight change, or other systemic symptoms warrant a general medical evaluation.
