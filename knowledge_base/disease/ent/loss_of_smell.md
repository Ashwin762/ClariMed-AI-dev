# Loss of Smell (Anosmia)

## Overview

Anosmia is the partial or complete loss of the sense of smell. It may
occur temporarily due to infections, allergies, or nasal blockage, or it
may be associated with neurological conditions or injury. Since smell
contributes significantly to taste, many individuals also notice reduced
taste perception.

**Note:** This condition is assessed primarily from reported
symptoms. It cannot be reliably diagnosed through image analysis.

---

# Common Symptoms

- Complete loss of smell

- Reduced ability to detect odors

- Reduced sense of taste

- Nasal congestion

- Runny nose

- Difficulty identifying familiar smells

---

# Causes

Loss of smell occurs when the smell receptors or nerves responsible for
detecting odors are affected.

### Common causes

Common cold, sinus infections, allergies, COVID-19, nasal polyps, head
injury, aging, neurological disorders, and smoking.

---

# Risk Factors

- Allergies

- Chronic sinus disease

- Smoking

- Viral infections

- Older age

- Head injury

---

# Severity Classification

## 🟢 Low

- Temporary loss of smell during a cold

- Mild nasal congestion

Recommendation: Monitor symptoms and consult an ENT specialist if they
persist.

---

## 🟡 Moderate

- Persistent loss of smell

- Reduced taste affecting appetite

Recommendation: Consult an ENT specialist for evaluation.

---

## 🔴 High

Any of the following:

- Sudden loss of smell after head injury

- Loss of smell with neurological symptoms

- Persistent unexplained anosmia

Recommendation: Seek urgent medical evaluation.

---

# Home Care

- Stay hydrated.

- Avoid smoking.

- Manage allergies appropriately.

- Maintain good nasal hygiene.

- Follow medical advice for underlying conditions.

---

# Prevention

- Treat allergies and sinus infections promptly.

- Avoid smoking.

- Wear protective equipment during activities with risk of head injury.

- Maintain good respiratory health.

---

# When to Consult a Doctor

Consult an ENT specialist if:

- Loss of smell lasts longer than two weeks.

- Taste is significantly affected.

- Symptoms occur without nasal congestion.

- Loss follows a head injury.
