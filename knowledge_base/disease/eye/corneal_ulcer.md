# Corneal Ulcer

## Overview

A corneal ulcer is an open sore on the cornea — the clear front surface of the eye — usually caused by infection or injury. It is a **potentially sight-threatening condition** that requires prompt medical attention, especially in contact lens wearers.

---

# Common Symptoms

- Significant eye pain
- Redness
- Blurred vision
- Sensitivity to light
- A visible white or grey spot on the cornea
- Excessive tearing or discharge
- Feeling of something in the eye

---

# Causes

### Infection

Bacterial, viral, or fungal infection of the cornea, often following minor trauma or contact lens overuse.

### Injury

Scratches, foreign bodies, or chemical exposure can lead to ulceration if infected.

---

# Risk Factors

- Extended or improper contact lens wear (including sleeping in lenses)
- Eye injury or trauma
- Dry eye disease
- Weakened immune system
- Prior corneal disease

---

# Severity Classification

## 🟡 Moderate

- Mild pain, small area of concern
- Vision not significantly affected

Recommendation: Prompt ophthalmology evaluation — do not wait, corneal ulcers can worsen quickly.

---

## 🔴 High — Possible Emergency

Any of the following:

- Significant eye pain
- Rapidly worsening vision
- Large or spreading white spot on the cornea
- Contact lens wearer with these symptoms

Recommendation: **Seek emergency eye care immediately.** Untreated corneal ulcers can cause permanent scarring and vision loss within a short time.

---

# Home Care

There is no safe home treatment for a suspected corneal ulcer. If you wear contact lenses, remove them immediately and do not reinsert until cleared by an eye doctor. Do not rub the eye. Seek professional care promptly rather than waiting.

---

# Prevention

- Never sleep in contact lenses unless specifically approved for extended wear.
- Follow proper contact lens hygiene (clean, replace, and store correctly).
- Avoid swimming or showering in contact lenses.
- Protect eyes from injury during high-risk activities.

---

# When to Consult a Doctor

Any suspected corneal ulcer warrants **same-day** ophthalmology evaluation — this is not a "wait and see" condition.

---

# Emergency Warning Signs

Seek emergency care immediately if:

- Significant or worsening eye pain
- Rapid vision changes
- A visible white/grey spot on the cornea
- Symptoms in a contact lens wearer

---
