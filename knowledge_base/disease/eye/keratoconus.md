# Keratoconus

## Overview

Keratoconus is a progressive condition where the normally round cornea gradually thins and bulges outward into a cone shape, distorting vision. It usually develops slowly over years, often starting in the teens or twenties, and progressively worsens vision that glasses can't fully correct. Left unmanaged, advanced cases can require specialized treatment or, rarely, a corneal transplant — but early detection and modern treatments (like corneal cross-linking) can halt progression effectively.

**Note:** The defining visual sign of this condition is the cornea's irregular *shape* — a conical bulge rather than its normal round curve. This is a geometric/contour feature, not a color or texture pattern, and cannot be reliably assessed by this tool's current image analysis (which measures color and texture statistics, not shape). This condition is therefore assessed from reported symptoms only, even though a photo was uploaded — the AI is not claiming to visually confirm this from color/texture alone.

---

# Common Symptoms

- Gradually worsening or distorted vision, not fully corrected by glasses
- Frequent changes in eyeglass prescription
- Increased sensitivity to light and glare
- Halos or streaking around lights, especially at night
- Double vision or "ghosting" in one eye
- Eye rubbing (both a possible contributing factor and a common habit in those affected)
- In advanced cases, a visibly conical bulge in the cornea

---

# Causes

### Progressive corneal thinning

The cornea's collagen structure weakens over time, causing it to bulge forward under normal eye pressure. The exact trigger is often unclear, but genetics and chronic eye rubbing are strongly linked.

---

# Risk Factors

- Family history of keratoconus
- Chronic eye rubbing
- Certain connective tissue disorders
- Allergic conditions causing frequent eye rubbing
- Onset typically in the teens to early thirties

---

# Severity Classification

## 🟡 Moderate

- Gradually worsening or distorted vision
- Frequent prescription changes
- Increased light sensitivity or halos
- No sudden change

Recommendation: See an ophthalmologist for corneal evaluation — early treatment (such as corneal cross-linking) can halt progression, so earlier diagnosis matters significantly.

---

## 🔴 High — Possible Emergency (Acute Hydrops)

Sudden, severe eye pain with a rapid worsening of vision and significant light sensitivity — in advanced keratoconus, the stretched cornea can suddenly swell (a complication called acute hydrops), causing abrupt symptoms distinct from the usual slow progression.

Recommendation: **Seek emergency eye care immediately.** Sudden severe symptoms in someone with known or suspected keratoconus need urgent evaluation.

---

# Home Care

- Avoid rubbing the eyes, which can worsen progression.
- Wear prescribed corrective lenses (glasses or specialty contact lenses) as directed.
- Protect eyes from excessive UV exposure.
- Manage underlying allergies that trigger eye rubbing.

Keratoconus requires proper diagnosis and monitoring by an eye specialist — it is not a condition to self-manage.

---

# Prevention

- Avoid habitual eye rubbing, especially if there's a family history.
- Manage eye allergies promptly rather than rubbing to relieve itching.
- Get regular eye exams, particularly with a family history of keratoconus.

---

# When to Consult a Doctor

Consult an ophthalmologist if:

- Vision is gradually worsening or distorting despite updated glasses
- Eyeglass prescriptions are changing unusually often
- Night vision is increasingly affected by halos or glare

---

# Emergency Warning Signs

Seek emergency eye care immediately if:

- Sudden, severe eye pain develops
- Vision rapidly worsens over hours
- Severe new light sensitivity appears abruptly
