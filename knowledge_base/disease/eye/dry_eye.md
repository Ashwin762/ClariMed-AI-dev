# Dry Eye Disease

## Overview

Dry Eye Disease (DED) is a multifactorial disorder of the tears and ocular surface caused by reduced tear production, excessive tear evaporation, or poor tear quality. It results in discomfort, visual disturbances, tear film instability, and may damage the ocular surface if left untreated.

Dry eye is one of the most common eye conditions and can significantly affect quality of life, especially during prolonged visual tasks such as reading or using digital devices.

---

# Types

## Aqueous Deficient Dry Eye

Occurs when the lacrimal glands produce insufficient tears.

Common causes:

- Age-related tear reduction
- Sjögren syndrome
- Lacrimal gland disorders
- Autoimmune diseases

---

## Evaporative Dry Eye

Occurs when tears evaporate too quickly.

Common causes:

- Meibomian gland dysfunction
- Blepharitis
- Prolonged screen time
- Reduced blinking
- Dry or windy environments

---

# Common Symptoms

- Dryness
- Burning sensation
- Stinging
- Gritty or sandy feeling
- Foreign body sensation
- Eye fatigue
- Redness
- Blurred vision
- Watery eyes (reflex tearing)
- Light sensitivity
- Difficulty wearing contact lenses

---

# Causes

Dry eye may result from:

- Reduced tear production
- Increased tear evaporation
- Meibomian gland dysfunction
- Aging
- Autoimmune diseases
- Certain medications
- Long hours of screen use
- Environmental conditions
- Previous eye surgery

---

# Risk Factors

- Increasing age
- Female sex
- Menopause
- Diabetes
- Vitamin A deficiency
- Autoimmune disorders
- Contact lens use
- Smoking
- Low humidity
- Air conditioning
- Prolonged computer or mobile phone use
- Certain medications (antihistamines, antidepressants, beta blockers, diuretics)

# Severity Classification

## 🟢 Mild

- Occasional dryness
- Mild irritation
- Symptoms occur mainly during prolonged screen use or environmental stress
- No significant visual impairment

Recommendation:

Home care and lifestyle modification.

---

## 🟡 Moderate

- Frequent dryness
- Burning sensation
- Eye fatigue
- Intermittent blurred vision
- Daily discomfort

Recommendation:

Consult an ophthalmologist.

---

## 🔴 Severe

- Persistent severe discomfort
- Constant blurred vision
- Corneal involvement
- Filamentary keratitis
- Severe ocular surface damage

Recommendation:

Urgent ophthalmology consultation.

# Home Care

- Blink frequently during screen use.
- Follow the 20-20-20 rule (every 20 minutes, look at something 20 feet away for 20 seconds).
- Stay hydrated.
- Increase indoor humidity when possible.
- Wear protective glasses in windy environments.
- Use lubricating eye drops only as advised by a healthcare professional.
- Limit exposure to smoke and dust.

---

# Prevention

- Take regular breaks while reading or using digital devices.
- Position computer screens slightly below eye level.
- Avoid direct air from fans or air conditioners.
- Maintain adequate hydration.
- Stop smoking and avoid second-hand smoke.
- Use wrap-around glasses in windy environments.
- Practice proper eyelid hygiene.

---

# When to Consult a Doctor

Consult an ophthalmologist if:

- Symptoms persist despite home care.
- Vision becomes blurry frequently.
- Artificial tears do not relieve symptoms.
- Eye pain develops.
- Redness becomes severe.
- Contact lenses become uncomfortable.
- Symptoms interfere with daily activities.

---

# Emergency Warning Signs

Seek immediate medical care if:

- Sudden vision loss
- Severe eye pain
- Chemical injury
- Eye trauma
- Severe corneal ulcer symptoms
- Rapid worsening of vision

# Patient Education

Dry Eye Disease is often a chronic condition that can usually be managed with lifestyle modifications, proper eye care, and treatment recommended by an ophthalmologist.

Long periods of screen use can worsen symptoms because blinking frequency decreases.

Managing environmental factors and maintaining eye lubrication are important parts of treatment.

---

# Frequently Asked Questions

## Is Dry Eye Disease contagious?

No.

Dry Eye Disease is not contagious.

---

## Can prolonged computer use cause dry eye?

Yes.

Extended screen time reduces blinking frequency, increasing tear evaporation and worsening symptoms.

---

## Can Dry Eye affect vision?

Yes.

Temporary blurred vision is common and usually improves after blinking or lubrication. Persistent vision changes require medical evaluation.

---

## Can I wear contact lenses?

Some people with dry eye experience discomfort while wearing contact lenses. An ophthalmologist can recommend suitable options if contact lenses are still appropriate.
