# Ringworm

## Overview

Ringworm (tinea corporis) is a fungal skin infection that produces a ring-shaped, scaly, itchy patch with a raised, well-defined border. Despite the name, it has nothing to do with worms — it's caused by fungi called dermatophytes.

---

# Common Symptoms

- Ring-shaped or circular patch
- Itching
- Scaly, flaky texture
- Redness, especially at the border
- Mild burning in some cases

---

# Causes

### Fungal infection

Dermatophyte fungi infect the outer layer of skin.

### Transmission

Spreads via direct skin contact, shared towels, combs, or contact with infected animals.

---

# Risk Factors

- Warm, humid environments
- Shared sports equipment or locker rooms
- Close contact with an infected person or pet
- Excessive sweating

# Severity Classification

## 🟢 Low

- Small, single patch
- Mild itching
- No spreading

Recommendation: Home care with antifungal treatment as advised, and monitoring.

---

## 🟡 Moderate

- Multiple patches or spreading area
- Persistent itching
- No improvement after 1–2 weeks of basic care

Recommendation: Consult a dermatologist.

---

# Home Care

- Keep the affected area clean and dry.
- Avoid sharing towels, clothing, or grooming tools.
- Wash bedding and clothing regularly in hot water.
- Avoid scratching, which can spread the infection to other areas.

---

# Prevention

- Keep skin dry, especially in skin folds.
- Avoid sharing personal items.
- Treat pets showing similar skin patches, as they can be a source.
- Wear breathable fabrics in humid weather.

---

# When to Consult a Doctor

Consult a dermatologist if:

- The patch doesn't improve within 2 weeks of basic care
- It spreads rapidly across the body
- It recurs frequently

---

# Emergency Warning Signs

Ringworm is rarely an emergency, but seek care if:

- Rapid spread across large body areas
- Signs of secondary bacterial infection (pus, increasing pain, fever)
