# Irritable Bowel Syndrome

## Overview

IBS is a common, chronic disorder affecting the large intestine, causing abdominal cramping, bloating, and changes in bowel habits. It doesn't damage the bowel or increase cancer risk, but it can significantly affect quality of life.

**Note:** This condition is assessed from reported symptoms only — there is no relevant photo, so the AI does not request an image for this body-part category.

---

# Common Symptoms

- Abdominal pain or cramping, often relieved after a bowel movement
- Bloating and excess gas
- Diarrhea, constipation, or alternating between the two
- Mucus in the stool
- Symptoms often triggered by food or stress
- Sensation of incomplete bowel emptying

---

# Causes

The exact cause isn't fully understood. Contributing factors include altered gut motility, increased gut sensitivity, changes in gut bacteria, and the gut-brain connection.

### Common triggers

Certain foods (dairy, wheat, some vegetables, carbonated drinks), stress, and hormonal changes.

---

# Risk Factors

- Age under 50
- Female sex (more commonly diagnosed)
- Family history of IBS
- Anxiety, depression, or high stress
- History of severe gut infection

---

# Severity Classification

## 🟢 Low

- Occasional bloating or cramping
- Bowel habits mostly regular
- Minimal impact on daily life

Recommendation: Identify triggers; dietary adjustments may help.

---

## 🟡 Moderate

- Frequent symptoms affecting work, study, or social life
- Persistent changes in bowel habits
- Symptoms not improving with dietary changes

Recommendation: Consult a gastroenterologist for evaluation and a management plan.

---

# Home Care

- Keep a food and symptom diary to identify triggers.
- Eat regular meals; don't skip meals.
- Increase fiber gradually (for constipation-predominant IBS).
- Stay well hydrated.
- Manage stress (exercise, relaxation techniques, adequate sleep).

---

# Prevention

IBS can't be prevented, but flares can be reduced by identifying and avoiding personal food and stress triggers, and maintaining regular eating and sleeping patterns.

---

# When to Consult a Doctor

Consult a gastroenterologist if:

- Symptoms are persistent and affecting daily life
- Dietary changes aren't helping
- You want a proper evaluation to rule out other conditions

**Important:** IBS is diagnosed partly by excluding other conditions. A doctor should confirm it rather than self-diagnosing.

---

# Emergency Warning Signs

These are NOT typical of IBS and require prompt evaluation:

- Blood in the stool
- Unintentional weight loss
- Persistent severe abdominal pain
- Symptoms starting after age 50
- Anemia or persistent fever
- Nighttime symptoms that wake you from sleep
