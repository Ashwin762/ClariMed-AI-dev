# Asthma

## Overview

Asthma is a chronic condition in which the airways narrow, swell, and produce extra mucus, making breathing difficult. Symptoms range from mild and occasional to severe attacks that are life-threatening. It's manageable with proper treatment but requires ongoing medical care.

---

# Common Symptoms

- Wheezing (a whistling sound when breathing out)
- Shortness of breath
- Chest tightness or pressure
- Coughing, often worse at night or early morning
- Symptoms triggered by exercise, allergens, or cold air

---

# Causes

### Airway hyperresponsiveness

Airways overreact to triggers, causing muscle tightening, swelling, and mucus production.

### Common triggers

Allergens (pollen, dust mites, pets), exercise, cold air, respiratory infections, smoke, and stress.

---

# Risk Factors

- Family history of asthma or allergies
- Personal history of eczema or allergic rhinitis
- Exposure to smoke or air pollution
- Childhood respiratory infections
- Obesity

---

# Severity Classification

## 🟢 Low

- Occasional mild wheezing
- Symptoms less than twice a week
- No night-time symptoms

Recommendation: Consult a doctor to establish a management plan; avoid known triggers.

---

## 🟡 Moderate

- Symptoms most days
- Night-time symptoms disrupting sleep
- Reliever inhaler needed frequently

Recommendation: Consult a pulmonologist — current treatment may need adjustment.

---

## 🔴 High — Possible Emergency

Any of the following:

- Severe breathlessness at rest
- Inability to speak full sentences
- Reliever inhaler not helping
- Bluish lips or fingertips
- Drowsiness or confusion

Recommendation: **Seek emergency care immediately.** A severe asthma attack is life-threatening.

---

# Home Care

Asthma requires prescribed medication (controller and reliever inhalers) directed by a doctor — there is no substitute for proper medical management. Supporting measures: identify and avoid triggers, follow your written asthma action plan, and use inhalers exactly as prescribed.

---

# Prevention

- Identify and avoid personal triggers.
- Take controller medication consistently, even when feeling well.
- Get an annual flu vaccination.
- Avoid smoke exposure.
- Maintain a healthy weight.

---

# When to Consult a Doctor

Consult a doctor or pulmonologist if:

- You experience recurrent wheezing or breathlessness
- You use a reliever inhaler more than twice a week
- Night-time symptoms disturb your sleep
- Symptoms limit your daily activities

---

# Emergency Warning Signs

Seek emergency care immediately if:

- Severe breathlessness or inability to speak in full sentences
- Reliever inhaler provides no relief
- Bluish lips, face, or fingertips
- Drowsiness, confusion, or exhaustion from the effort of breathing
