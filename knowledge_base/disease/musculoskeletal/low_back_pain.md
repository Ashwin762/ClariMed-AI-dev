# Low Back Pain

## Overview

Low back pain is extremely common and usually mechanical in origin — related to muscles, ligaments, or joints rather than serious disease. Most episodes improve substantially within a few weeks with activity and simple measures.

**Note:** This condition is assessed from reported symptoms only — there is no reliable visual sign in a standard photo, so the AI does not request an image for this body-part category.

---

# Common Symptoms

- Aching or stiffness across the lower back
- Pain that worsens with certain movements or prolonged sitting
- Muscle spasm or tightness
- Reduced flexibility
- Pain sometimes radiating into the buttock or thigh

---

# Causes

### Mechanical causes

Muscle or ligament strain, poor posture, prolonged sitting, or lifting incorrectly.

### Other causes

Disc problems, arthritis of the spine, and (less commonly) nerve compression.

---

# Risk Factors

- Sedentary lifestyle
- Poor lifting technique
- Obesity
- Prolonged sitting or driving
- Weak core muscles
- Older age

---

# Severity Classification

## 🟢 Low

- Mild ache, no radiating pain
- Movement largely unaffected
- Improving within days

Recommendation: Stay gently active; avoid prolonged bed rest.

---

## 🟡 Moderate

- Persistent pain beyond 2-4 weeks
- Pain affecting sleep or daily activities
- Some radiation into the leg

Recommendation: Consult an orthopedist or physiotherapist.

---

## 🔴 High — Possible Emergency

Any of the following ("red flags"):

- Loss of bladder or bowel control
- Numbness in the groin or inner thighs (saddle anaesthesia)
- Progressive leg weakness
- Severe pain following a significant injury
- Unexplained weight loss or fever with back pain

Recommendation: **Seek emergency medical care immediately.** These may indicate cauda equina syndrome or another serious condition.

---

# Home Care

- Stay gently active — prolonged bed rest slows recovery.
- Apply heat or cold for symptomatic relief.
- Continue light daily activities as pain allows.
- Pain relief as advised by a doctor or pharmacist.
- Gentle stretching and walking.

---

# Prevention

- Strengthen core and back muscles.
- Practice good lifting technique (bend the knees, not the back).
- Maintain a healthy weight.
- Take breaks from prolonged sitting.
- Ensure good posture and workstation setup.

---

# When to Consult a Doctor

Consult an orthopedist if:

- Pain persists beyond 4-6 weeks
- Pain radiates below the knee
- Pain significantly disrupts sleep or daily function
- Episodes recur frequently

---

# Emergency Warning Signs

Seek emergency care immediately if:

- Loss of bladder or bowel control
- Numbness in the groin or inner thigh area
- Progressive weakness in one or both legs
- Back pain following major trauma
- Fever or unexplained weight loss with back pain

---

# Recommended Specialist

Orthopedist (or Physiotherapist for rehabilitation; emergency care if red flags present)
