# Gout

## Overview

Gout is a form of inflammatory arthritis caused by a buildup of uric acid crystals in a joint, most classically the big toe. It causes sudden, severe attacks of pain, swelling, and redness, often starting at night.

**Note:** This condition is assessed from reported symptoms only — there is no reliable visual sign in a standard photo, so the AI does not request an image for this body-part category.

---

# Common Symptoms

- Sudden, intense joint pain, often starting overnight
- Swelling and redness around the joint
- Warmth in the affected joint
- Extreme tenderness — even light touch (like a bedsheet) can be painful
- Most commonly affects the big toe, but can affect ankles, knees, and other joints
- Symptoms typically peak within 12-24 hours

---

# Causes

### Uric acid crystal buildup

High levels of uric acid in the blood form sharp crystals in a joint, triggering intense inflammation.

### Contributing factors

Diet high in purines (red meat, seafood, alcohol), obesity, dehydration, certain medications, and kidney function issues.

---

# Risk Factors

- Diet high in red meat, seafood, or alcohol (especially beer)
- Obesity
- Family history of gout
- Kidney disease
- Certain medications (diuretics)
- Dehydration

---

# Severity Classification

## 🟡 Moderate

- Moderate joint pain and swelling
- First episode or infrequent recurrence
- Able to manage with rest and pain relief

Recommendation: Consult a doctor promptly — gout is very treatable, and recurrence is preventable with proper management.

---

## 🔴 High — Possible Emergency

Any of the following:

- Fever accompanying the joint symptoms (may indicate a joint infection rather than gout — needs urgent differentiation)
- Severe, unbearable pain
- Signs of spreading infection (warmth, redness extending beyond the joint)

Recommendation: **Seek prompt medical care.** A hot, swollen joint with fever needs urgent evaluation to rule out infection.

---

# Home Care

- Rest and elevate the affected joint.
- Apply ice to reduce swelling and pain.
- Stay well hydrated.
- Avoid alcohol and purine-rich foods during a flare.
- Pain relief as advised by a doctor — some common pain relievers may not be appropriate for gout.

---

# Prevention

- Limit red meat, organ meats, and shellfish.
- Limit alcohol, especially beer.
- Stay well hydrated.
- Maintain a healthy weight.
- Take prescribed uric-acid-lowering medication consistently if prescribed.

---

# When to Consult a Doctor

Consult an orthopedist or general physician if:

- This is your first suspected gout attack (needs confirmation)
- Attacks are recurring
- Pain is severe or not improving with home care

---

# Emergency Warning Signs

Seek prompt medical care if:

- Fever accompanies the joint pain and swelling
- Signs of spreading infection around the joint
- Pain is severe and unbearable

---

# Recommended Specialist

Orthopedist (or Rheumatologist for long-term management)
