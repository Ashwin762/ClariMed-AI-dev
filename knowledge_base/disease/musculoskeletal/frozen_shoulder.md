# Frozen Shoulder

## Overview

Frozen shoulder is a condition causing progressive pain and stiffness in the shoulder joint, significantly limiting movement. It develops gradually over months and typically progresses through freezing, frozen, and thawing stages before resolving, though full recovery can take one to three years.

**Note:** This condition is assessed from reported symptoms only — there is no reliable visual sign in a standard photo, so the AI does not request an image for this body-part category.

---

# Common Symptoms

- Gradually worsening shoulder pain
- Progressive stiffness and limited range of motion
- Difficulty raising the arm overhead or reaching behind the back
- Pain often worse at night, disrupting sleep
- Stiffness that may partially improve over many months (thawing stage)

---

# Causes

### Capsule thickening and tightening

The connective tissue surrounding the shoulder joint thickens and tightens, restricting movement. The exact trigger is often unclear.

### Contributing factors

Prolonged immobility (after injury or surgery), diabetes, and thyroid disorders increase risk.

---

# Risk Factors

- Diabetes (significantly increases risk)
- Thyroid disorders
- Prolonged shoulder immobility (post-injury, post-surgery)
- Age 40-60
- More common in women

---

# Severity Classification

## 🟢 Low

- Mild stiffness
- Pain manageable
- Most movements still possible

Recommendation: Gentle range-of-motion exercises; monitor progression.

---

## 🟡 Moderate

- Significant stiffness limiting daily activities
- Pain disrupting sleep
- Difficulty with overhead or behind-the-back movements

Recommendation: Consult an orthopedist for a structured treatment plan, which may include physiotherapy.

---

# Home Care

- Gentle range-of-motion exercises as tolerated (avoid forcing painful movements).
- Apply heat before stretching, cold after activity for pain relief.
- Pain relief as advised by a doctor or pharmacist.
- Stay as active as pain allows — complete immobility can worsen stiffness.

---

# Prevention

- Keep the shoulder moving after any injury or surgery, as advised by a doctor.
- Manage diabetes and thyroid conditions well.
- Avoid prolonged immobilization of the shoulder where possible.

---

# When to Consult a Doctor

Consult an orthopedist if:

- Shoulder stiffness is progressively worsening
- Pain is disrupting sleep or daily activities
- Range of motion is significantly limited

---

# Emergency Warning Signs

Frozen shoulder is not typically an emergency. However, seek prompt care if shoulder pain follows a fall or injury with visible deformity, or if sudden severe weakness develops.

---

# Recommended Specialist

Orthopedist (or Physiotherapist for rehabilitation)
