# Dry Mouth (Xerostomia)

## Overview

Dry mouth, also known as xerostomia, is a condition in which the
salivary glands do not produce enough saliva to keep the mouth
adequately moist. Saliva plays an important role in digestion, speaking,
swallowing, and protecting the teeth from decay. Persistent dry mouth
may increase the risk of dental problems and oral infections.

**Note:** This condition is assessed primarily from reported
symptoms. Visible signs may be present, but image analysis alone is not
sufficient for diagnosis.

---

# Common Symptoms

- Dry or sticky feeling in the mouth

- Difficulty chewing or swallowing

- Dry throat

- Cracked lips

- Bad breath

- Thick or stringy saliva

- Difficulty speaking

- Increased thirst

---

# Causes

Dry mouth occurs when saliva production decreases.

### Common causes

Certain medications, dehydration, diabetes, radiation therapy, smoking,
autoimmune disorders, and aging.

---

# Risk Factors

- Older age

- Diabetes

- Smoking or tobacco use

- Certain medications

- Autoimmune diseases

- Dehydration

---

# Severity Classification

## 🟢 Low

- Mild mouth dryness

- Occasional thirst

Recommendation: Increase water intake and maintain good oral hygiene.

---

## 🟡 Moderate

- Persistent dry mouth

- Difficulty eating or speaking

- Frequent bad breath

Recommendation: Consult a dentist for evaluation.

---

## 🔴 High

Any of the following:

- Severe difficulty swallowing

- Mouth sores with infection

- Difficulty speaking due to dryness

- Persistent symptoms despite hydration

Recommendation: Seek prompt medical or dental evaluation.

---

# Home Care

- Drink water frequently.

- Avoid tobacco and alcohol.

- Chew sugar-free gum if appropriate.

- Maintain good oral hygiene.

- Avoid caffeinated beverages if they worsen symptoms.

---

# Prevention

- Stay well hydrated.

- Maintain good oral hygiene.

- Visit the dentist regularly.

- Avoid smoking.

- Manage underlying medical conditions.

---

# When to Consult a Doctor

Consult a dentist if:

- Dry mouth persists for several weeks.

- You develop frequent mouth sores.

- Difficulty swallowing increases.

- Tooth decay becomes frequent.
