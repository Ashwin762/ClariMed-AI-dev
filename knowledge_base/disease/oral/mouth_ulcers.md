# Mouth Ulcers

## Overview

Mouth ulcers (aphthous ulcers) are small, painful sores that develop on the soft tissues inside the mouth — often the inner cheeks, lips, or under the tongue. They are common, not contagious, and usually heal on their own within one to two weeks.

---

# Common Symptoms

- Small, round or oval sore with a white or yellow center and red border
- Pain, especially when eating or talking
- Burning or tingling sensation before the ulcer appears
- Mild swelling around the sore

---

# Causes

### Minor trauma

Accidental cheek/lip bites, aggressive brushing, or dental work.

### Other triggers

Stress, certain foods (acidic/spicy), vitamin deficiencies (B12, iron, folate), and hormonal changes.

---

# Risk Factors

- Stress or lack of sleep
- Nutritional deficiencies
- Braces or ill-fitting dental appliances
- Family history of frequent ulcers

# Severity Classification

## 🟢 Low

- Single small ulcer
- Mild discomfort

Recommendation: Home care; usually resolves within 1–2 weeks.

---

## 🟡 Moderate

- Multiple ulcers or large ulcer (>1cm)
- Pain significantly affecting eating
- Recurs frequently

Recommendation: Consult a dentist if ulcers are unusually large, numerous, or don't heal within 2–3 weeks.

---

# Home Care

- Rinse with warm salt water several times a day.
- Avoid spicy, acidic, or rough-textured foods while healing.
- Use a soft-bristled toothbrush.
- Over-the-counter oral gels can help with discomfort (as advised).

---

# Prevention

- Avoid known trigger foods if identified.
- Manage stress levels.
- Ensure adequate intake of B12, iron, and folate.
- Use a soft toothbrush and be gentle during brushing.

---

# When to Consult a Doctor

Consult a dentist or doctor if:

- An ulcer lasts longer than 2–3 weeks
- Ulcers are unusually large or numerous
- Ulcers recur very frequently
- Accompanied by fever or feeling generally unwell

---

# Emergency Warning Signs

Seek prompt care if:

- Difficulty swallowing or breathing
- High fever accompanying widespread mouth sores
