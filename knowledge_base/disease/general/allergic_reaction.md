# Allergic Reaction

## Overview

An allergic reaction occurs when the immune system overreacts to a normally harmless substance — food, medication, insect stings, or environmental triggers. Reactions range from mild skin symptoms to anaphylaxis, a rapid, life-threatening reaction requiring emergency treatment.

---

# Common Symptoms

- Hives (raised, itchy welts on the skin)
- Itchy rash
- Mild swelling (localized)
- Sneezing, runny nose (if inhaled allergen)
- Nausea (if food allergy)

### Severe (anaphylaxis) symptoms — EMERGENCY

- Swelling of the face, lips, tongue, or throat
- Difficulty breathing or wheezing
- Throat tightness or a feeling of the throat closing
- Rapid pulse
- Dizziness or fainting
- Widespread hives with breathing difficulty

---

# Causes

### Immune hypersensitivity

The immune system releases histamine and other chemicals in response to an allergen it mistakenly identifies as harmful.

### Common triggers

Foods (nuts, shellfish, eggs), medications (especially antibiotics), insect stings, latex, and environmental allergens.

---

# Risk Factors

- Known allergies (personal or family history)
- Asthma or other allergic conditions (higher anaphylaxis risk)
- Previous allergic reactions (each reaction can potentially be more severe than the last)

---

# Severity Classification

## 🟢 Low

- Mild hives or itching
- No swelling of the face/throat
- No breathing difficulty

Recommendation: Antihistamine as advised by a pharmacist/doctor; monitor for worsening.

---

## 🟡 Moderate

- Widespread hives
- Mild facial swelling without breathing difficulty
- Persistent symptoms despite antihistamines

Recommendation: Prompt medical evaluation.

---

## 🔴 High — Emergency (Possible Anaphylaxis)

Any of the following:

- Swelling of the face, lips, tongue, or throat
- Difficulty breathing or wheezing
- Throat tightness
- Dizziness or fainting
- Rapid pulse with widespread hives

Recommendation: **This is a medical emergency.** Use an epinephrine auto-injector if available and prescribed, and seek emergency care immediately — anaphylaxis can become life-threatening within minutes.

---

# Home Care

For mild reactions only: remove/avoid the trigger, use an antihistamine as advised by a pharmacist, and apply a cool compress for itching. Any facial swelling or breathing symptoms need emergency care, not home management.

---

# Prevention

- Identify and strictly avoid known allergens.
- Carry a prescribed epinephrine auto-injector if you have a history of severe reactions.
- Inform healthcare providers of known drug allergies before any treatment.
- Wear a medical alert bracelet if you have severe allergies.

---

# When to Consult a Doctor

Consult a doctor for any new or unexplained allergic reaction to identify the trigger and discuss whether an epinephrine auto-injector should be prescribed for future protection.

---

# Emergency Warning Signs

**Seek emergency care immediately if:**

- Swelling of the face, lips, tongue, or throat
- Difficulty breathing, wheezing, or throat tightness
- Dizziness, fainting, or rapid heartbeat
- Widespread hives combined with any of the above

---

General Physician (emergency care for anaphylaxis; may refer to an Allergist)
