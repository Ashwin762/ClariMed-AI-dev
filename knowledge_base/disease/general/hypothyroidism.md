# Hypothyroidism

## Overview

Hypothyroidism occurs when the thyroid gland doesn't produce enough thyroid hormone, which slows down many of the body's processes. It develops gradually, and symptoms are often mistaken for normal aging, stress, or just "being tired" — which is why it commonly goes undiagnosed for years. It's straightforward to test for and treat once identified.

# Common Symptoms

- Persistent, unexplained fatigue
- Unexplained weight gain
- Increased sensitivity to cold
- Dry skin and hair thinning
- Constipation
- Low mood or difficulty concentrating
- Muscle weakness or aches
- Puffiness in the face
- Irregular or heavy menstrual periods

---

# Causes

### Autoimmune thyroiditis (Hashimoto's)

The most common cause — the immune system gradually attacks the thyroid gland.

### Other causes

Iodine deficiency, certain medications, prior thyroid surgery or radiation treatment, or pituitary gland problems.

---

# Risk Factors

- Female sex (significantly more common in women)
- Family history of thyroid disease
- Age over 60
- Other autoimmune conditions
- Prior thyroid surgery or radiation to the neck

---

# Severity Classification

## 🟢 Low

- Mild fatigue or subtle symptoms
- No significant impact on daily life

Recommendation: Mention symptoms at your next routine checkup; a simple blood test can screen for this.

---

## 🟡 Moderate

- Multiple symptoms present together (fatigue, weight gain, cold intolerance, dry skin)
- Affecting daily energy or mood

Recommendation: See a doctor for thyroid function testing — diagnosis is a simple blood test, and treatment is usually a once-daily medication.

---

# Home Care

Hypothyroidism requires proper diagnosis and hormone replacement treatment — it cannot be effectively managed with home care alone. While awaiting evaluation: maintain regular sleep, balanced nutrition, and gentle activity as tolerated.

---

# Prevention

There's no reliable way to prevent most cases, since the leading cause is autoimmune. Ensuring adequate dietary iodine and getting screened if you have risk factors or a family history are the most practical steps.

---

# When to Consult a Doctor

Consult a doctor if you notice a cluster of these symptoms together, especially unexplained fatigue with weight gain and cold intolerance — this combination is a reasonable and common reason to request a thyroid function test.

---

# Emergency Warning Signs

Hypothyroidism itself is rarely an emergency, but seek prompt care if severe, longstanding untreated hypothyroidism causes confusion, very low body temperature, or slowed breathing (a rare complication called myxedema coma) — this would typically follow years of undiagnosed, severe disease rather than appear suddenly.

---

# Recommended Specialist

General Physician (may refer to an endocrinologist for ongoing management)
